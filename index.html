Hook Review

A few possible openings for the same course.

Each hook emphasizes a different part of the same product: family trust, manipulation defense, rabbit holes, and the limits of parental controls.

The product idea: a self-paced internet self-defense course for families, built from short parent-child missions, parent scripts, answer keys, and a Digital Safety Plan.

Hook A

Your kid is growing up in the most confusing information environment humans have ever built.

What it is: a self-paced family course built around four manipulation patterns kids can learn to spot.

AI images, fake screenshots, viral rumors, fake experts, wellness influencers, predatory strangers, and algorithmic rabbit holes are already shaping what kids believe. Teach them how not to get fooled, isolated, or trapped online before it is too late.

Hook B

Your kid is more likely to come to you if you have already talked about the hard stuff.

What it is: a four-mission course parents do with kids ages 10-13.

Online threats work by making kids feel trapped, ashamed, or alone. This course gives your family a shared language before the first scary message, fake screenshot, or private-chat pressure shows up.

Hook C

The internet can turn a lonely afternoon into a whole worldview.

What it is: a family course that helps kids recognize online communities built around grievance, secret knowledge, humiliation, and belonging.

Kids do not have to be looking for anything dangerous to get pulled toward it. A joke, a rumor, a video, or a bad week can become a feed full of people explaining who to blame, who not to trust, and why the kid has finally found the people who understand. This course teaches the pattern before the pattern becomes an identity.

Hook D

Do not get played by the internet.

What it is: short internet self-defense missions for kids and families.

Screenshots can lie. Influencers can sell fake proof. Strangers can manufacture trust. Algorithms can turn one bad mood into a whole identity. Learn the tricks before they work on you.

Hook E

Parental controls can block a website. They cannot teach your kid what to do next.

What it is: a practical course that teaches judgment, scripts, and recovery plans.

Blocking tools matter, but they do not help when a rumor spreads in a group chat, a fake expert sounds convincing, or a stranger moves the conversation somewhere private. Families need practiced judgment, not surveillance alone.
